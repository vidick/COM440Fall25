\begin{enumerate}

\item {(*, $\blacklozenge$)\bf Coin flipping from bit commitment} \\
Suppose given a scheme for bit commitment that is $\eps_h$-hiding (i.e. Bob can guess Alice's commited bit with probabilitiy at most $\frac12+\eps_h$) and $\eps_b$ binding (i.e. the maximum probabilities with which Alice can force a $0$ or a $1$ outcome, for a given commitment phase, satisfy $p_0+p_1\leq 1+\eps_b$). 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Design a simple protocol for coin-flipping, using the bit commitment scheme. 
\item Determine the maximum bias of the coin flipping protocol, for both cheating Alice and cheating Bob, as a function of $\eps_h$ and $\eps_b$. 
\end{enumerate}

\item {(*)\bf Different flavors of oblivious transfer} \\
The variant of oblivious transfer that we saw in class is called $1$-out-of-$2$ OT, because Bob obtains one out of two possible Alice inputs. (For this problem, we consider only the case $\ell=1$, i.e. Alice has two bits as inputs.) We can consider a couple other variants: 
\begin{itemize}
\item Rabin OT: Alice transmits a bit $b$ to Bob, who receives $b$ with probability $1/2$ while
Alice does not know which is the case. That is, the output of Bob is either $b$ or $\bot$
(indicating that the bit was not received).
\item $1$-out-of-$k$ OT for $k > 2$: Alice holds $k$ bits $b_1,\ldots,b_k$. For $c\in\{1,\ldots, k\}$ of Bobâ€™s
choice, he can learn $b_c$ but none of the others, and Alice does not learn $c$.
\end{itemize}
Prove the equivalence of these three variants, by providing the following reductions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $1$-out-of-$k$ OT $\implies$ $1$-out-of-$2$ OT
\item $1$-out-of-$2$ OT $\implies$ $1$-out-of-$k$ OT \emph{[Hint: In your protocol, the sender should choose $k$ random bits and invoke the $1$-out-of-$2$ OT protocol $k$ times.]}
\item $1$-out-of-$2$ OT $\implies$ Rabin OT
\item Rabin OT $\implies$ $1$-out-of-$2$ OT \emph{[Hint: Use Rabin OT to send sufficiently many random bits. In your protocol, the
receiver might learn both bits, but with negligible probability only.]}
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
